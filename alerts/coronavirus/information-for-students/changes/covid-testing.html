Free end of semester Covid tests for students with no symptoms

Students who wish to take a Covid-19 test before returning home for Christmas can now book slots through our new booking system.

Tests are only available to students who do not have symptoms of Covid-19 (‘Asymptomatic Testing’). They are also available to students who have previously had Covid-19.

Tests are simple, fast and free of charge. They are voluntary but we strongly encourage all students who wish to return home safely to take them - it will help to stop the spread of the virus and protect your family and friends. You can also take tests if you are planning to stay in Oxford over the festive period, or if you commute to the University each day.

We strongly encourage you to take two tests, three days apart, with the second test taking place just before you plan to travel home. You will need to allow time for your second test result to come through before you leave (tests will be rapidly processed on site; you may receive your results in as little as an hour, however it could take longer). If it is not possible to take two tests, please book at least one test.

International students and those travelling overseas

If you are travelling overseas during the festive period, you may be required to show evidence of a negative Covid-19 PCR test prior to travel.

Please note that the Asymptomatic Test (lateral flow test) we are offering is NOT the same as a PCR test. 

If you need a PCR test for travel purposes you should organise this with a private provider. Check the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office website for details of country entry requirements to see which test you will require.


Book your tests


Test results

Booking your tests

You can book your tests using our simple online booking system. Testing is available at our Headington (at the Sports Hall at Brookes Sport, next to Cheney Student Village), Harcourt Hill, Swindon and Wheatley campuses over the coming weeks.

When booking your testing slots, you should bear in mind when you want to travel home. You should take the second test just before you plan to travel. If your second test is negative you should travel home as soon as possible and avoid unnecessary socialising.

Tests will be rapidly processed on site. You may receive your results in as little as an hour, however it could take longer.

Book your tests

How do I book a test?

You can book your test using our simple online booking system. Testing is available at our Headington, Harcourt Hill, Swindon and Wheatley campuses.

You should book two tests, three days apart. If this is not possible, please book at least one test. 

Do I need to pay?

No, the tests are free of charge.

If you need a ‘fit to fly’ certificate, you will need to organise this with a private Covid test provider and there is likely to be a cost involved.

If I have symptoms of Covid-19 what test do I book?

This test is aimed at identifying Covid-19 infection in those without symptoms (asymptomatic). If you have symptoms of Covid-19, please self-isolate and go online or call 119 to book a free diagnostic test. You should inform the University using the new Coronavirus reporting form.

What if I develop symptoms after booking my test but before the test date?

You must immediately self-isolate and then book an NHS test online or by calling 119. You should cancel your booking on the booking system so that it can be allocated to someone else.

Where can I get my test?

We will have testing available at our Headington (at the Sports Hall at Brookes Sport, next to Cheney Student Village), Harcourt Hill, Swindon and Wheatley campuses.

Do I have to get a test before I can return home?

Tests are not compulsory but we strongly encourage all students who wish to return home safely to take them - it will help to stop the spread of the virus and protect your family and friends. You can also take tests if you are planning to stay in Oxford over the festive period, or if you commute to the University each day.

About the test

The test is self-administered - you will do it yourself at one of our testing facilities on campus, with support from a testing helper. The test involves rubbing a swab (similar to a long cotton bud) at the back of your throat and up your nostril. 

Book your tests

How long will it take?

The test should take about 10 minutes, however you should allow additional time for registration and in case of queuing. Please remember to bring your mobile phone and student ID with you, for registration and results purposes.

How many tests should I take? When should I take them?

You should ideally take two tests, three days apart, before travelling home from University. If this is not possible, you should take at least one test.

You should take the second test just before you plan to travel, and should aim to travel as soon as possible after receiving the second, negative test.

Tests will be rapidly processed on site; you may receive your results in as little as an hour, however it could take longer. Please bear this in mind when you book your testing slot and plan your travel home. 

Why should I take two tests? Isn’t one enough?

Although they give a result much more quickly, the lateral flow tests are not as sensitive as the PCR tests (that is, lateral flow tests cannot detect very low levels of Covid-19). By doing two tests, three days apart, the programme is more likely to identify anyone with Covid-19 than from a single test.

I’m self-isolating as a close contact - can I attend my booked test?

If you are a close contact of a confirmed case, you can attend your booked test but you must then immediately return to your accommodation to self-isolate. You should avoid travelling on public transport. If you have no other option than to use public transport, you should strictly observe social distancing measures, wear a face covering and wash hands thoroughly and regularly during the journey. 

What will happen at the testing centre?

The test is self-administered - you will do it yourself at one of our testing facilities on campus, with support from a testing helper. The test involves rubbing a swab (similar to a long cotton bud) at the back of your throat and up your nostril. 

What should I bring with me to the testing centre?

You should bring your mobile phone and student ID with you - this is for registration and results purposes. Please also remember to bring your face covering.

Where are the tests taking place?

We will have testing available at our Headington (at the Sports Hall at Brookes Sport, next to Cheney Student Village), Harcourt Hill, Swindon and Wheatley campuses. 

What are the tests?

The tests we are providing are a ‘lateral flow test’ that is quick to take and gives results quickly. This detects the coronavirus antigen that is produced when a person is infectious with coronavirus.

The test is self-administered - you will do it yourself at one of our testing facilities on campus, with support from a testing helper. The test involves rubbing a swab (similar to a long cotton bud) at the back of your throat and up your nostril.

What sort of test is it?

There are two main types of test used to check if people currently have coronavirus.

The test that is being offered in this programme is called a lateral flow antigen test. This detects the coronavirus antigen that is produced when a person is infectious with coronavirus. These are quicker tests that produce a rapid result and do not require a laboratory to be processed.

If you’ve previously booked a test through the NHS, it is likely to have been a PCR test, and this looks for the virus’s genetic material (ribonucleic acid or RNA). These tests are currently more commonly used in the NHS for testing people with symptoms. They require a laboratory to be processed.

Will this test mean I can enter another country/be ‘fit to fly’?

If you are travelling overseas during the festive period, you may be required to show evidence of a negative Covid-19 PCR test prior to travel. Please note that the Asymptomatic Test (lateral flow test) we are offering is NOT the same as a PCR test.

If you need a PCR test for travel purposes you should organise this with a private provider. You will need to pay for this service.

You should also check the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office website for details of country entry requirements to see which test you will require and any other conditions of entry currently in place.

Why should I get tested if I have no symptoms?

You can still be infectious and not show or experience any symptoms, which means you could pass the virus to others. The lateral flow test is designed to detect the level of virus in individuals who do not experience and show any symptoms (they are ‘asymptomatic’). By taking a test, you will help to stop the spread of the virus, protect other people and save lives. 

Why should I get tested - if I test positive I’ll have to self-isolate?

It is really important that we all do our bit to stop the spread of the virus and protect our friends, loved ones and other members of society. Taking the test will help us fight the pandemic and save lives. Stopping all contact with other people for a limited period of time to prevent them from becoming infected is an essential part of the fight against the virus.

I’ve already had Covid-19 - should I take this test?

The tests are recommended for all individuals without Covid-19 symptoms, including those who have previously tested positive for Covid-19 and completed their self-isolation 10 days ago or more.

Is the test safe?

Lateral flow tests are validated technology (the most commonly known type is a pregnancy test), they are safe and the results are trusted. These tests have undergone rigorous testing and evaluation including at Public Health England’s research laboratories to ensure they are verified for use. 

Will the test hurt?

It may be a little uncomfortable, but it shouldn’t hurt. 

How accurate is the test?

Lateral flow tests are very accurate (highly specific), which means that only a very small proportion of people who do not have coronavirus will receive a positive result (false positive). However, in mass testing, because so many people without symptoms are being tested, there is still the possibility of getting a false positive result. If you receive a positive test result, you should book a PCR test to confirm the result either by booking a test online or by calling 119.

I have additional needs, will someone be available to help me?

Assisted testing is available for anyone who is unable to self administer the test– including assistance with the swabbing of their throat or nose, or help with reading instructions. If an assisted test is required, please let us know via the online booking system and inform staff upon arrival, when completing registration.

Please note that disabled access bays are available at all sites. All testing sites are accessible to wheelchair users.

What happens if I’m exempt from wearing a face covering?

Appropriate face coverings need to be worn throughout the testing process whilst in the testing facility, apart from when guided by a testing professional to remove it when you take your sample by swabbing. You should put your face covering back on immediately after completing your test and before leaving the testing booth.

Should you have age, health or disability reasons for not wearing a face covering, please inform the site manager as soon as you arrive so that they can take additional precautions. 

Test results

You will receive a message with your results either via the NHS Test & Trace app, by text or email, depending on which details you provided when you registered. Tests will be rapidly processed on site; you may receive your results in as little as an hour, however it could take longer.

Once you have received two negative tests, you are advised to return home as soon as possible and avoid unnecessary socialising.

Anyone whose test returns a positive outcome will still need to self-isolate and book an NHS (PCR) test in accordance with current government guidance. Please also inform Oxford Brookes using the Coronavirus Reporting Form. See the Test and trace, reporting and self-isolation webpage for more guidance on this.

If you test negative but are identified as a close contact of someone who has tested positive, you can return home (from 3 December when the national lockdown ends and ideally by 9 December at the end of the student travel window) using private transport wherever possible but you must continue self-isolation at home for a period of 14 days. If you have no other option than to use public transport, you should strictly observe social distancing measures, wear a face covering and wash hands thoroughly and regularly during the journey.

Book your tests

How will I get my results?

Your test result will be passed to NHS Test and Trace. You will receive a message from them with your results via the NHS Test & Trace app, text or email, depending on which details you provided when you registered for your tests.

Can I wait after the test to get my results?

No, you cannot wait onsite to get your results for infection control reasons. 

How soon will I get my results?

Tests will be rapidly processed on site; you may receive your results in as little as an hour, however it could take longer. If you do not receive your test results within 24 hours, please call 119 and enquire. In most cases you will be asked to retake the lateral flow test.

Who else will receive my results?

A copy of your result will be sent to your GP. If you have tested positive, a notification will be sent to Public Health England.

Oxford Brookes and NHS Test and Trace take the protection of your privacy very seriously. To find out how your personal data will be used when you register for a coronavirus test read the Government’s coronavirus privacy note.

What happens once I have two negative test results?

If you receive two negative tests, you are advised to return home as soon as possible and avoid unnecessary socialising. Remember that you should not return to your permanent, home address before 3 December as the UK remains under national lockdown restrictions.

If you test negative but are identified in the meantime as a close contact of someone who has tested positive, you can return home (from 3 December when the national lockdown ends and ideally by 9 December at the end of the student travel window) but must continue self-isolation at home for a period of 14 days, and you should use private transport wherever possible to get home. If you have no other option than to use public transport, you should strictly observe social distancing measures, wear a face covering and wash hands thoroughly and regularly during the journey.

What happens if my test is positive?

If your test result is positive you will need to isolate immediately. You should then book an NHS (PCR) test to confirm if you have Covid. You should self-isolate for 10 days from your first positive test. You should inform the University using the new Coronavirus reporting form if you receive a positive test. See the Test and trace, reporting and self-isolation webpage for more guidance. 

What happens if my test is negative, but I’m a close contact of someone who has tested positive?

If you test negative but are identified as a close contact of someone who has tested positive, you can return home (from 3 December when the national lockdown ends and ideally by 9 December at the end of the student travel window) using private transport wherever possible and you must continue self-isolation at home for a period of 14 days. If you have no other option than to use public transport, you should strictly observe social distancing measures, wear a face covering and wash hands thoroughly and regularly during the journey.

What happens if the result is unclear?

If we can’t read the sample it means it’s not possible to say if you were infectious when the test was done.

If this is the case, you will need to take the same tests again as soon as possible. If this is the second time you’ve got this result, you will need to book a different test – follow the advice you were given when you were tested or go to www.gov.uk/get-coronavirus-test.

You only need to self-isolate if:

  • you have any symptoms of coronavirus (a high temperature, a new, continuous cough or a loss or change to your sense of smell or taste)
  • you’ve tested positive for coronavirus - this means you have coronavirus
  • you live with someone who has symptoms or tested positive
  • someone in your support bubble has symptoms or tested positive
  • you’re told to self-isolate by NHS Test and Trace or the NHS COVID-19 app
  • you arrive in the UK from a country with a high coronavirus risk.

If I test positive, will people I live with need to self-isolate?

If you have a positive test, anyone you live with must self-isolate until 14 days after the date of your first test or after your symptoms began (if earlier), unless they are participating in the specific trial of regular testing for contacts – in which case they will be given specific instructions on what they can and cannot do during this period. 

If I test positive, will my contacts be traced?

If you have a positive test result, your contacts will be traced by NHS Test and Trace. They will be required to self-isolate until 14 days after their most recent contact with you, unless they are participating in the specific trial of regular testing for contacts – in which case they will be given specific instructions on what they can and cannot do during this period.

If you have a positive Asymptomatic Test (lateral flow test) but get a negative result on the NHS PCR test, your contacts do not need to be traced [See What sort of test is it? for details on the different types of test].

Why do I need to maintain social distancing if I’ve had a negative test?

After a negative test, an individual is still susceptible to becoming infected and spreading the virus. Respecting distancing when it is needed and other rules is the most powerful way of stopping the spread of the virus.


I have Covid-19

When to travel

Students should not plan to travel before 3 December due to the national restrictions. Once you have received a second negative result, you should aim to travel as soon as possible and avoid unnecessary socialising.

The ‘student travel window’ of 3-9 December has been put in place to enable students to return home safely to family and friends. Should you need to use public transport to return home, please observe social distancing measures, wear a face covering and wash your hands regularly during your journey.

Students may travel after 9 December and we will have testing available to allow you to do this. 

Book your tests

I can’t travel home in the student travel window of 3-9 December. What do I do?

We know that some students are on placements or have work commitments which mean they cannot travel by 9 December. Testing will be available until 14 December to enable you to get a test before you leave. You will then need to travel as safely as possible. 

Can I travel home on public transport?

We ask students to travel home as safely as possible, remembering the national lockdown restricts travel until 2 December and the ‘student travel window’ is between 3-9 December. You should observe social distancing measures, wear a face covering and wash your hands regularly during your journey.

If you are symptomatic or have tested positive for Covid-19, you should not travel until you have been tested and/or advised to do so by NHS Test & Trace. 

What if I test positive and have to stay longer? Can I stay in my university accommodation?

Those who are already licenced to reside in our halls of residence can remain in the accommodation as this is included in the licence term. If you require additional support during this time, please reach out to your halls team. If you are staying in private accommodation and need a room to isolate in we can also help with this. Please contact the Accommodation Bureau to discuss this further.
